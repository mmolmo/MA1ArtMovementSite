Abstract Expressionism

What is it? A Brief History

Abstract expressionism is an American Art movement originating in New York City in the 1940s. Artists in this movement were affected by the horror of World War II and rejected painting human subjects in favor of an abstract style. In response to the tragedy, these artists created the movement as an exploration of pouring raw emotions and feelings onto the canvas.

Key Artists

  • Jackson Pollock: ‘Action painter’ characterized by intense splashes of color in drips and a busy feeling through the work.
  • Lee Krasner: Wife to Pollock, her work is also in the action painting subgenre and uses black, white, red, and green hues.
  • Barnett Newman: ‘Color field painter’, his works are large canvas of a single color designed to overwhelm the viewer.
  • Mark Rothco: ‘Color field painter’, his works are large saturated blocks of different color hues designed to overwhelm the viewer
  • Willem de Kooning: ‘Action Painter’ characterized by chaotic brush strokes and spots of color

Influences

Many artists in the movement were influenced by famous psychologists such as Carl Jung and his theory of the subconscious mind, which suggested that people have a subconscious schema formed as a response to their experiences. Other major contributions include the art movements of Surrealism and Cubism. Surrealism was an art movement with major characteristics being unexpected combinations of subject matter and nonsensical or absurd storylines. Cubism was another art movement that depicted subjects with broken-up geometric forms in an abstract manner. All of these influences showed themselves in the works of the Abstract expressionists, which can be characterized as chaotic, nonsensical, rebellious, and intensely emotional.

Legacy

Abstract Expressionism died out in the 60's but remain highly influential. It affected movements such as Color Field painting, Lyrical Abstraction, Fluxus, Pop Art, Minimalism, Postminimalism, Neo-expressionism, and more. Movements were also made in direct responses to it include Hard-edge painting and Pop Art.
